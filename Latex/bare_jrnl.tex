% --- Abstract ---
\begin{abstract}
In the growing literature on machine learning for concrete strength prediction, ensemble frameworks combining feature engineering, multiple base models, and constrained fusion have been proposed as avenues toward higher accuracy. However, the marginal value of each added architectural component is rarely quantified. This study constructs ACDCB (Age-Conditioned Dual-Space Constrained Blending) as a controlled experimental vehicle---not as a recommended prediction framework---and conducts a systematic component-wise dissection on two independent datasets: the UCI concrete benchmark ($N = 1{,}030$) and a Mexican concrete dataset ($N = 4{,}420$) with non-destructive testing variables. On UCI, controlled ablation reveals that the appearance of strong performance relative to the AdaBoost baseline ($\Delta R^2 = +0.033$) is almost entirely attributable to replacing AdaBoost with gradient-boosted trees---not to feature engineering, dual-space design, age-conditioned piecewise blending, or constrained optimization. A refined feature-space analysis stratified by physical-basis level finds that the purest physically derived features (Level~A: water-cement ratio, water-binder ratio, and maturity transforms) provide a small positive contribution ($\Delta R^2 = +0.0018$), but this is eroded and eventually reversed as mechanically weaker features are introduced, confirming that the original 32-dimensional space is dominated by noise-inducing collinearities. Non-linear meta-learners (Random Forest, MLP) are evaluated and found to underperform linear SLSQP stacking on both datasets, confirming that the failure of ensembling is rooted in cross-model prediction redundancy rather than methodological restriction to linear fusion. The age-conditioned piecewise blending yields an $R^2$ gain of $3 \times 10^{-5}$ and a threshold scan across $\tau \in [3, 180]$ days confirms complete insensitivity. SLSQP constrained optimization is validated as a genuine safeguard: under artificially degraded model pools, unconstrained OLS produces negative weights while SLSQP correctly clips them to the simplex, though the constraint remains non-binding in well-calibrated settings. On the Mexican dataset, the pattern is identical: the AdaBoost baseline already reaches $R^2 = 0.9936$ because ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV) serves as a direct physical strength proxy, all complex components contribute $\Delta R^2 \leq 0.002$, and a single tuned XGBoost model achieves $R^2 = 0.9954$, RMSE~$= 0.6464$\,MPa. Crucially, a De-UPV ablation on the Mexican dataset confirms that even after removing UPV and all non-destructive testing variables, the dual-space architecture and age-conditioned piecewise blending contribute effectively zero marginal gain ($\Delta R^2 < 0.0001$), and raw features again match or exceed engineered representations. A fine-tuned single XGBoost model operating on raw features outperforms the full ACDCB framework on both datasets with lower computational cost and higher interpretability. We conclude that for small-to-medium tabular datasets in structural engineering, rigorous hyperparameter optimization of a single strong learner constitutes the empirical performance ceiling, and that additional architectural complexity should be adopted only after demonstrating statistically significant marginal gain across independent validation data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==========================================================================

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} accurate prediction of concrete compressive strength is a cornerstone problem in structural engineering, with direct implications for construction scheduling, quality control, and service-life assessment \cite{yeh1998concrete}. Traditional approaches rely on empirical regression formulas---such as Abrams' law or power-law maturity models---that offer computational simplicity but cannot adequately capture the high-dimensional, nonlinear interactions among mix design variables, admixture effects, and curing conditions \cite{yeh2006analysis}.

Over the past two decades, machine learning (ML) methods have emerged as powerful alternatives. Artificial neural networks (ANN), support vector machines (SVM), and ensemble methods---particularly AdaBoost with decision tree weak learners---have demonstrated progressively improving predictive accuracy on the UCI benchmark dataset \cite{yeh1998concrete,chou2014machine}. Notably, the AdaBoost-based approach reported by Feng et al.\ \cite{feng2020machine} achieved a 10-fold cross-validated $R^2$ of approximately 0.95 and RMSE below 5\,MPa, establishing a strong baseline for subsequent work.

\subsection{The Complexity Escalation Problem}

A discernible trend in recent civil engineering ML literature is the proliferation of increasingly complex prediction frameworks: multi-stage ensembles, dual-stream architectures, physics-informed feature engineering pipelines, and constrained optimization layers are combined into composite models whose individual components are rarely evaluated in isolation \cite{chou2014machine,erdal2013prediction}. While each additional component is motivated by plausible reasoning---feature engineering encodes domain knowledge, multiple base models capture complementary patterns, age-conditioning reflects hydration kinetics, and constrained optimization prevents instability---the \emph{marginal contribution} of each component to predictive accuracy is seldom quantified through rigorous ablation.

This trend raises a methodological concern: \textbf{are we over-engineering prediction models for datasets whose inherent complexity may be adequately captured by a single, well-tuned learner?} On the UCI concrete dataset ($N = 1{,}030$, $D = 8$ base variables), gradient-boosted trees with default hyperparameters already achieve $R^2 > 0.92$. The headroom for further improvement is bounded by dataset noise, measurement error, and the fundamental predictability limit of concrete strength from bulk mix proportions alone. In this regime, architectural complexity may introduce additional estimation variance without commensurate bias reduction---a manifestation of the bias-variance tradeoff in small-sample settings.

\subsection{Research Questions and Approach}

This study subjects a representative complex ensemble framework---ACDCB (Age-Conditioned Dual-Space Constrained Blending)---to systematic component-wise ablation to answer four questions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Feature engineering}: Does expanding 8 raw variables to 32 engineered features improve prediction, or does collinearity-induced variance dominate?
\item \textbf{Multi-model ensemble}: Do three gradient-boosted tree variants (XGBoost, LightGBM, HGB) provide complementary predictions, or are they functionally redundant?
\item \textbf{Age-conditioned piecewise blending}: Does partitioning the dataset at 28 days produce meaningfully different fusion weights and improved predictions?
\item \textbf{Constrained optimization}: Does simplex-constrained SLSQP optimization yield different weights or predictions compared to unconstrained ordinary least squares?
\end{enumerate}

To answer these questions, we construct ACDCB following its original design specification and then progressively disable each component, measuring the resulting change in 10-fold cross-validated $R^2$ and RMSE. We stress that ACDCB is not proposed as a recommended prediction framework; rather, it is deliberately constructed as a controlled experimental vehicle to isolate the marginal contribution of each architectural component commonly advocated in the civil engineering ML literature. We further benchmark the full framework against a single XGBoost model with hyperparameters optimized via Bayesian search on the raw 8-dimensional features. An independent Mexican concrete dataset ($N = 4{,}420$) is introduced to test whether the ablation patterns generalize beyond the specific characteristics of the UCI benchmark.

Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Empirical}: A comprehensive ablation study demonstrating that three of ACDCB's four architectural components---feature engineering, model pool diversity, and age-conditioned piecewise blending---contribute negligible or negative marginal gain on the UCI dataset. The sole substantive improvement over the AdaBoost baseline is driven by the replacement of AdaBoost with modern gradient-boosted trees. These findings are cross-validated on an independent 4{,}420-sample Mexican concrete dataset with qualitatively identical results.

\item \textbf{Methodological}: A detailed diagnosis of \emph{why} each component fails: feature collinearity (15 pairs with $|r| > 0.90$) inflates variance; cross-model prediction correlations exceeding 0.996 render the multi-model pool redundant; and the age-threshold performance landscape is completely flat across $\tau \in [3, 180]$ days. Bootstrap analysis and age-stratified error decomposition provide additional resolution on the sources of residual error.

\item \textbf{Prescriptive}: Evidence that a single XGBoost model, tuned via 200-trial Bayesian optimization on raw features, achieves $R^2 = 0.9523$ and RMSE $= 3.65$\,MPa on UCI and $R^2 = 0.9954$, RMSE $= 0.6464$\,MPa on the Mexican dataset---outperforming the full ACDCB framework on both datasets with lower computational complexity and full interpretability. We propose that rigorous single-model optimization should serve as the mandatory baseline before additional architectural complexity is adopted.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~II describes the experimental databases, including the UCI benchmark and the independent Mexican concrete validation dataset. Section~III presents the mathematical foundations of the baseline models and the ACDCB framework. Section~IV details the implementation and training strategy. Section~V reports model validation and evaluation results. Section~VI presents the ablation study---the core of this work---decomposing component contributions, diagnosing failure modes, and reporting cross-dataset validation. Section~VII provides comparative analysis against prior work. Section~VIII discusses the implications for model design in structural engineering ML, including measurement noise ceiling analysis and age-stratified error decomposition, and Section~IX concludes.

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}

Beyond the 8 base variables, two tiers of engineered features are constructed to capture known concrete mechanics relationships.

\paragraph{Tier 1 --- Mechanistic Features}
Shared by primary and anchor spaces, 14 features. Let $\varepsilon = 10^{-6}$ for numerical stability. Define intermediate quantities:
\begin{align}
B &= C + S + FA \quad \text{(total binder)} \label{eq:binder}\\
TA &= CA + FA_g \quad \text{(total aggregate)} \label{eq:agg}\\
t_{\log} &= \log(1 + t) \label{eq:tlog}
\end{align}

The mechanistic features include:
\begin{align}
&\frac{W}{C},\; \frac{W}{B},\; \frac{SP}{B},\; \frac{S + FA}{B},\; \frac{FA_g}{TA} \notag\\
&t_{\log},\; \sqrt{t},\; t^{0.25} \notag\\
&\text{Abrams Index}^{\dagger} = \frac{t_{\log}}{W/B},\; C \cdot t_{\log},\; B \cdot t_{\log},\; \frac{W}{B} \cdot t_{\log} \notag\\
&\text{Paste Index}^{\dagger} = \frac{C + S + FA + W + SP}{TA} \notag
\end{align}

These features are not equally grounded in established concrete mechanics. Water-binder ratio has direct support from Abrams' law, whereas several interaction or index terms are hypothesis-driven constructs introduced only to test whether tree models can exploit derived quantities. \subsection{Mexican Concrete Dataset (Independent Validation)}

To assess the generalizability of the ablation findings beyond the UCI benchmark, we introduce an independent dataset of Mexican concrete experiments. This dataset comprises $N = 4{,}420$ experimental observations, drawn from parametric studies involving CEMEX and Apasco cement brands with the natural additive Opuntia ficus indica (nopal cactus). The target variable is the 28-day compressive strength $C_s$ (MPa), with $\mu = 31.19$\,MPa, $\sigma = 9.59$\,MPa, and skewness $= -0.034$, indicating a near-symmetric distribution---in contrast to the UCI dataset's pronounced right skew (skewness $>2.0$).

The dataset features 4 categorical and 7 numerical input variables. The numerical features include cement content, water content, fine and coarse aggregate, age, ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV, m/s), electrical resistivity (Er, k$\Omega\cdot$cm), tensile strength (Ts, MPa), and flexural strength (Fs, MPa). Age spans 3 to 120 days across 8 discrete levels (3, 7, 14, 28, 56, 90, 120 days), with well-balanced sampling: minimum 160 specimens at 3 days and maximum 940 at 28 days. This balanced age distribution contrasts sharply with the UCI dataset, where the right-skewed age profile concentrates most samples at early ages.

A critical difference from the UCI dataset is the presence of UPV as a non-destructive testing (NDT) proxy. Ultrasonic pulse velocity is directly correlated with concrete stiffness and compressive strength, providing a strong physical signal that dramatically elevates the baseline predictability. As demonstrated in the cross-dataset ablation results (Section~\ref{sec:cross_dataset}), this single feature drives the AdaBoost baseline to $R^2 = 0.9936$, compressing the headroom for improvement to less than $0.007$ in $R^2$.

This dataset differs from the UCI benchmark in three substantive dimensions: (1) \textbf{geography and materials}---Mexican cementitious systems with a natural organic additive versus internationally sourced mixes; (2) \textbf{feature space}---the inclusion of NDT measurements (UPV, Er, Ts, Fs) alongside standard mix proportions; and (3) \textbf{sample size and age balance}---4{,}420 samples with near-uniform age representation versus 1{,}030 samples with a heavy skew. These differences make the Mexican dataset an ideal independent validation target for testing whether the ablation patterns observed on UCI generalize beyond the specific data characteristics of the original benchmark.

\section{Methodology}
%==========================================================================

The ACDCB framework described below is constructed for the purpose of systematic ablation, embodying four architectural strategies whose individual and combined effects are tested in Section~VI.

\subsection{Baseline Models}

To establish comparative baselines, we reproduce three representative models from the literature.

\subsubsection{AdaBoost Regression}

AdaBoost (Adaptive Boosting) is an ensemble method that sequentially trains weak learners---here, Classification and Regression Trees (CART)---with adaptive sample weighting \cite{freund1997decision}. For a training set $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{m}$, the algorithm proceeds as follows:

\textbf{Step 1 --- Weight initialization}: Each sample receives equal weight $w_{1,i} = 1/m$.

\textbf{Step 2 --- Iterative training}: For iteration $k = 1, \dots, K$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Train weak learner $G_k(\mathbf{x})$ on the weighted dataset.
\item Compute sample-wise relative error:
\begin{equation}
e_{k,i} = \frac{|y_i - G_k(\mathbf{x}_i)|}{E_k}, \quad E_k = \max_i |y_i - G_k(\mathbf{x}_i)|
\end{equation}
\item Compute the aggregate error and weight coefficient:
\begin{equation}
e_k = \sum_{i=1}^{m} w_{k,i} \cdot e_{k,i}, \quad \alpha_k = \frac{e_k}{1 - e_k}
\end{equation}
\item Update sample weights for the next iteration:
\begin{equation}
w_{k+1,i} = \frac{w_{k,i} \cdot \alpha_k^{1 - e_{k,i}}}{\sum_{j=1}^{m} w_{k,j} \cdot \alpha_k^{1 - e_{k,j}}}
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Step 3 --- Ensemble prediction}: The final prediction is the weighted median of all weak learner outputs, with learner weight $\ln(1/\alpha_k)$.

\subsubsection{Artificial Neural Network}

The ANN baseline employs a multi-layer perceptron with one hidden layer of 8 neurons and ReLU activation, following the architecture used in Yeh \cite{yeh1998concrete}:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{h} &= \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}^{(1)}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^{(1)}) \\
\hat{y} &= \mathbf{W}^{(2)}\mathbf{h} + b^{(2)}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{W}^{(1)} \in \mathbb{R}^{8 \times 8}$, $\mathbf{W}^{(2)} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 8}$. Training minimizes MSE via stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with momentum $\beta = 0.5$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{W} \leftarrow \mathbf{W} - \eta \nabla_{\mathbf{W}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} + \beta \Delta\mathbf{W}_{\text{prev}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Support Vector Regression}

SVR with radial basis function (RBF) kernel maps inputs to a high-dimensional feature space and fits an $\varepsilon$-insensitive tube:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf{w}, b, \xi, \xi^*} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_{i=1}^{m}(\xi_i + \xi_i^*)
\end{equation}
subject to:
\begin{align}
y_i - \mathbf{w}^T\phi(\mathbf{x}_i) - b &\leq \varepsilon + \xi_i \notag\\
\mathbf{w}^T\phi(\mathbf{x}_i) + b - y_i &\leq \varepsilon + \xi_i^* \notag\\
\xi_i, \xi_i^* &\geq 0 \notag
\end{align}
The RBF kernel is defined as $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \exp(-\gamma\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|^2)$.

\subsection{Gradient-Boosted Tree Base Models}

The ACDCB model pool comprises three gradient boosting variants, selected for their complementary optimization strategies and proven performance on tabular data.

\subsubsection{XGBoost}

XGBoost \cite{chen2016xgboost} employs a regularized objective with second-order Taylor expansion:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{(k)} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[g_i f_k(\mathbf{x}_i) + \frac{1}{2}h_i f_k^2(\mathbf{x}_i)\right] + \Omega(f_k)
\end{equation}
where $g_i = \partial_{\hat{y}^{(k-1)}} l(y_i, \hat{y}^{(k-1)})$ and $h_i = \partial^2_{\hat{y}^{(k-1)}} l(y_i, \hat{y}^{(k-1)})$ are the first- and second-order gradients of the loss, and:
\begin{equation}
\Omega(f_k) = \gamma T + \frac{1}{2}\lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + \alpha\|\mathbf{w}\|_1
\end{equation}
combines leaf-count penalty ($\gamma T$), L2 regularization, and L1 regularization.

\subsubsection{LightGBM}

LightGBM \cite{ke2017lightgbm} introduces gradient-based one-side sampling (GOSS) and exclusive feature bundling (EFB) for efficiency. Its leaf-wise tree growth strategy with depth constraints yields:
\begin{equation}
\text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{(\sum_{i \in L} g_i)^2}{\sum_{i \in L} h_i + \lambda} + \frac{(\sum_{i \in R} g_i)^2}{\sum_{i \in R} h_i + \lambda} - \frac{(\sum_{i \in P} g_i)^2}{\sum_{i \in P} h_i + \lambda}\right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Histogram-based Gradient Boosting (HGB)}

HGB (scikit-learn implementation) bins continuous features into integer-valued histograms ($B \leq 255$ bins), reducing split-finding complexity from $\mathcal{O}(n)$ to $\mathcal{O}(B)$:
\begin{equation}
\text{split\_gain}(j, s) = \frac{\sum_{i: x_{ij} \leq s} g_i}{\sum_{i: x_{ij} \leq s} h_i + \lambda} + \frac{\sum_{i: x_{ij} > s} g_i}{\sum_{i: x_{ij} > s} h_i + \lambda}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Dual Feature Spaces (D)}

Given the 8-dimensional base input $\mathbf{x}_{\text{base}} \in \mathbb{R}^8$, two feature transformation functions are defined:
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\text{primary}}: \mathbb{R}^8 \to \mathbb{R}^{32}, \quad \phi_{\text{anchor}}: \mathbb{R}^8 \to \mathbb{R}^{22}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_{\text{primary}}$ applies Tier~1 $+$ Tier~2 engineering (32 output dimensions), and $\phi_{\text{anchor}}$ applies Tier~1 only (22 output dimensions). The design rationale is:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Primary space} ($\mathbb{R}^{32}$): Maximizes predictive capacity through enriched interaction terms, compositional ratios, and nonlinear age transforms.
\item \textbf{Anchor space} ($\mathbb{R}^{22}$): Retains core mechanistic features while omitting fine-grained compositional decompositions, reducing estimation variance. Serves as a stabilization signal during blending.
\end{itemize}

The two spaces are \textbf{not} treated as competing alternatives but as \textbf{complementary views} of the same underlying physical process, to be reconciled through constrained fusion.

Under $K$-fold cross-validation ($K = 10$), each model $M_j$ produces out-of-fold (OOF) predictions $\hat{y}_i^{(j)}$ for every sample $i$. These are concatenated into the OOF prediction matrix:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{P} = \begin{bmatrix}
\hat{y}_1^{(1)} & \hat{y}_1^{(2)} & \cdots & \hat{y}_1^{(M)} \\
\hat{y}_2^{(1)} & \hat{y}_2^{(2)} & \cdots & \hat{y}_2^{(M)} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\hat{y}_N^{(1)} & \hat{y}_N^{(2)} & \cdots & \hat{y}_N^{(M)}
\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}, \quad M = 4
\end{equation}

The OOF matrix is critical: it provides an unbiased estimate of each model's generalization performance, preventing overfitting in the subsequent weight optimization stage.

\subsubsection{Constrained Weight Optimization (C, B)}

The blending weight vector $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^M$ is obtained by solving the constrained optimization problem:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf{w}} \; \text{RMSE}(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{P}\mathbf{w}) = \min_{\mathbf{w}} \; \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(y_i - \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j \hat{y}_i^{(j)}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
subject to the convex constraint set:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W} = \left\{\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^M \; \middle| \; w_j \geq 0 \;\; \forall j, \;\; \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j = 1\right\}
\end{equation}

The optimization is performed using Sequential Least Squares Quadratic Programming (SLSQP) \cite{kraft1988software}, a quasi-Newton method suitable for constrained nonlinear objectives. The convex constraints guarantee:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Interpretability}: Each $w_j$ is the fractional contribution of model $M_j$ to the ensemble.
\item \textbf{Stability}: The simplex constraint prevents unbounded extrapolation.
\item \textbf{Sparsity encouragement}: Non-negativity combined with the simplex can drive poorly performing models to zero weight, effecting implicit model selection.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Age-Conditioned Piecewise Blending (A, C)}

The dataset is partitioned at a physiochemically meaningful threshold $\tau = 28$ days:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_e = \{i \mid t_i \leq \tau\}, \quad \mathcal{D}_l = \{i \mid t_i > \tau\}
\end{equation}

Separate weight vectors $\mathbf{w}_e$ and $\mathbf{w}_l$ are optimized for each regime:
\begin{align}
\mathbf{w}_e &= \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{W}} \text{RMSE}(\mathbf{y}_e, \mathbf{P}_e\mathbf{w}) \\
\mathbf{w}_l &= \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{W}} \text{RMSE}(\mathbf{y}_l, \mathbf{P}_l\mathbf{w})
\end{align}

The final prediction for a new sample with age $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases}
\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{M} w_{e,j} \cdot M_j(\mathbf{x}), & t \leq 28 \text{ days} \\[12pt]
\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{M} w_{l,j} \cdot M_j(\mathbf{x}), & t > 28 \text{ days}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The 28-day threshold is chosen because it represents the standard curing period in structural concrete practice (ACI~318, EN~206), after which hydration kinetics transition from rapid early-age strength gain to asymptotic long-term development.

\subsubsection{Strategy Selection}

Between the global blend (single $\mathbf{w}$ for all ages) and the piecewise blend, selection follows a hierarchical rule:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compare $R^2$ values; prefer the strategy with higher $R^2$.
\item If $|R^2_{\text{global}} - R^2_{\text{piecewise}}| < \delta$ (tolerance $\delta = 5 \times 10^{-4}$), select the strategy with lower RMSE.
\end{enumerate}

This ``$R^2$-first, RMSE-tiebreaker'' rule prioritizes explained variance while using RMSE for fine discrimination.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Four complementary metrics are employed to assess model performance.

\textbf{Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$):}
\begin{equation}
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \bar{y})^2}
\end{equation}
$R^2$ measures the proportion of variance explained, with 1 indicating perfect prediction. It is scale-invariant and serves as our primary metric.

\textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE):}
\begin{equation}
\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}
\end{equation}
RMSE penalizes large errors quadratically, making it sensitive to outliers. Units: MPa.

\textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE):}
\begin{equation}
\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_i - \hat{y}_i|
\end{equation}
MAE provides a linear error measure, less sensitive to outliers than RMSE. Units: MPa.

\textbf{Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE):}
\begin{equation}
\text{MAPE} = \frac{100\%}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left|\frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i}\right|
\end{equation}
MAPE expresses error relative to the true value, facilitating cross-dataset comparisons.

\subsection{Diagnosis 1: Feature Engineering Introduces Harmful Collinearity}

The contribution of feature engineering is isolated by comparing V0 (AdaBoost, raw features) to V1 (3 GBDT models, engineered features). However, this comparison conflates two simultaneous changes: replacing AdaBoost with GBDT \emph{and} expanding the feature space from 8 to 32 dimensions. To disentangle these effects, we compare:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Algorithm upgrade (AdaBoost $\to$ GBDT)}: A single XGBoost on 8 raw features achieves $R^2 = 0.9523$, while AdaBoost achieves $R^2 = 0.9142$. The algorithm change alone accounts for $\Delta R^2 = +0.0381$.
\item \textbf{Feature engineering (8D $\to$ 32D)}: XGBoost on 32 engineered features achieves $R^2 = 0.9469$, which is \emph{lower} than XGBoost on 8 raw features ($R^2 = 0.9523$). The feature engineering step contributes $\Delta R^2 = -0.0054$---a net \emph{negative} effect.
\end{itemize}

Fig.~\ref{fig:p2_collinearity} reveals the mechanism behind this deterioration: among the 24 engineered features, 15 pairs exhibit Pearson correlations exceeding $|r| > 0.90$. For example, the Abrams index and maturity index are perfectly collinear ($r = 1.000$), as are the supplementary cementitious material ratio and cement fraction in binder ($r = -1.000$). Three age transformations (log, sqrt, 0.25-power) are inter-correlated at $r > 0.98$. In gradient boosting, each additional feature introduces a new split candidate; when features are highly collinear, the gain in bias reduction from additional splits is offset by increased estimation variance, particularly in the small-sample regime ($N = 1{,}030$).

Stepwise feature ablation confirms this diagnosis: a default HGB model achieves $R^2 = 0.9387$ on the full 32D space, $R^2 = 0.9406$ after removing the 10 Tier-2 features (22D anchor space), and $R^2 = 0.9413$ on the 8 raw features. \textbf{Removing engineered features improves performance.}

\subsection{Diagnosis 3: Age-Conditioned Piecewise Blending Is Unnecessary}

The 28-day age threshold is motivated by concrete maturity physics---hydration kinetics differ between early and late ages. However, a systematic scan of threshold candidates $\tau \in \{3, 7, 14, 21, 28, 56, 90, 180\}$ days reveals a completely flat performance landscape (Fig.~\ref{fig:p2_threshold_flatness}):

\begin{itemize}
\item $R^2$ range across thresholds: $4.2 \times 10^{-4}$ (max 0.94984 at $\tau = 14$ days, min 0.94942 at $\tau = 180$ days)
\item RMSE range: 0.016\,MPa (min 3.7396 at $\tau = 14$ days, max 3.7553 at $\tau = 56$ days)
\item $\Delta R^2$ (global blend vs.\ best threshold): $+4.4 \times 10^{-4}$
\end{itemize}

Any choice of $\tau$ between 3 and 180 days yields essentially identical performance. The age-dependent information that motivated the piecewise design is already captured by gradient-boosted trees through their native ability to learn age-conditioned splits. The explicit age partitioning provides no additional modeling capacity.

While a hard age boundary at $\tau = 28$ days could in principle introduce a prediction discontinuity at $t=\tau$, the threshold scan demonstrates that any $\tau \in [3,180]$ yields near-identical performance ($R^2$ range $=4.2 \times 10^{-4}$). This flatness has a direct implication: the discontinuity concern is secondary because the piecewise design itself provides no discriminable benefit. If the split point does not matter empirically, neither does the exact discontinuity induced by that split; the piecewise strategy is structurally unnecessary rather than merely poorly calibrated.

Moreover, the early-age and late-age weight vectors differ only modestly: HGB\_Anchor dominates both regimes (early: 0.633, late: 0.415), while XGBoost's weight increases from 0.314 (early) to 0.463 (late). The weight shift, while directionally consistent with the expectation that different models may specialize by age regime, does not translate into measurable predictive improvement.

\paragraph{Soft Age-Gating via Sigmoid Weighting.} A potential objection to the foregoing analysis is that hard binary partitioning introduces an artificial discontinuity that could mask genuine age-dependent specialization. To test this, sigmoid soft-weighting was evaluated on both datasets. The blending weight for the early-age model is defined as $w_e(t) = \sigma(\kappa(\tau - t))$, with the late-age weight $w_l(t) = 1 - w_e(t)$, where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the logistic function and $\kappa \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0\}$ controls transition sharpness ($\kappa \to \infty$ recovers the hard threshold).

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{UCI:} All soft-weighting variants performed at or below the hard threshold. The $\Delta R^2$ relative to the hard threshold ranged from $-0.000063$ to $-0.000110$, and the variance across $\kappa$ values was negligible.
\item \textbf{Mexican dataset:} Soft weighting performed essentially identically to the hard threshold, with $\Delta R^2 = +0.000001$.
\end{itemize}

The conclusion is decisive: even data-driven smooth transitions in the age-gating mechanism provide no measurable predictive gain. The failure of age-conditioning is not an artifact of the hard threshold choice but reflects the fact that gradient-boosted trees already learn age-dependent splits natively. \subsection{Diagnosis 4: Constrained Optimization Is a Safety Mechanism, Not a Performance Driver}

The simplex constraint ($w_i \geq 0$, $\sum w_i = 1$) is motivated by stability concerns: unconstrained linear stacking can produce negative weights that exploit error cancellation between models, potentially degrading under distribution shift. However, on this dataset with well-calibrated GBDT predictions, the constraint proves non-binding.

Comparing the SLSQP-constrained fusion (V2) against unconstrained ordinary least squares (V5):
\begin{itemize}
\item V2 (constrained): $R^2 = 0.9487$, RMSE $= 3.7000$\,MPa
\item V5 (unconstrained): $R^2 = 0.9487$, RMSE $= 3.6994$\,MPa
\item $\Delta R^2 = -2.6 \times 10^{-5}$, $\Delta$RMSE $= 0.0006$\,MPa
\end{itemize}

The OLS solution yields weights $[0.340, 0.023, 0.047, 0.589]$---all positive and summing to 0.999. No negative weights appear. The SLSQP solution differs only in the third decimal place. The constraint functions as a benign safety mechanism rather than a performance enabler: it guarantees interpretability and physical plausibility without measurably affecting predictive accuracy. Its value would manifest only under conditions where unconstrained stacking produces pathological weights (e.g., with severely miscalibrated or biased base models), which do not occur in this setting.

\subsection{Summary: The Real Performance Driver}

The ablation hierarchy, ranked by $\Delta R^2$ contribution, is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GBDT replaces AdaBoost}: $\Delta R^2 \approx +0.038$---the single substantive gain
\item \textbf{Dual-space anchor model}: $\Delta R^2 = +0.0012$---marginal ($\sim$0.1\%)
\item \textbf{Age-conditioned piecewise}: $\Delta R^2 = +3 \times 10^{-5}$---negligible
\item \textbf{Feature engineering}: $\Delta R^2 = -0.0054$---counterproductive
\item \textbf{Constrained optimization}: $\Delta R^2 \approx 0$---neutral
\end{enumerate}

In total, the four architectural innovations of ACDCB contribute a combined $\Delta R^2 < -0.004$ beyond what a single fine-tuned XGBoost on raw features achieves. The appearance of strong performance relative to the AdaBoost baseline is an artifact of the baseline's weakness, not the framework's strength.

\section{Discussion}
%==========================================================================

\subsection{When Do Extra Strategies Help? Boundary Conditions of the Findings}

The ablation results do not imply that feature engineering, model ensembling, age-conditioning, or constrained optimization are universally ineffective. Rather, they identify the \emph{boundary conditions} under which these strategies fail to provide marginal value:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Feature engineering} is harmful when the engineered features are algebraically derived from the same small set of base variables, creating near-perfect collinearities. It becomes valuable when features encode genuinely independent information (e.g., from different measurement modalities or physical simulations) that is not already captured by base variables.

\item \textbf{Multi-model ensembling} provides negligible gains when base models produce highly correlated predictions. The heterogeneous MLP/GPR test shows that lowering the minimum pairwise correlation to approximately 0.97 is still insufficient on this dataset; practically useful ensembling requires not merely different model names, but complementary errors strong enough to offset weaker individual accuracy.

\item \textbf{Age-conditioned piecewise blending} is unnecessary when the conditioning variable (age) is already a continuous input to all base models. Gradient-boosted trees learn age-dependent splits natively; explicit partitioning only adds value when the conditioning variable is \emph{not} available as a model input, or when the relationship between the conditioning variable and optimal model selection is stepwise rather than smooth.

\item \textbf{Constrained optimization} serves as a safety mechanism whose value materializes when unconstrained stacking produces pathological weights---a scenario more likely with miscalibrated, biased, or overfitted base models. In well-regularized settings, it is benign but non-contributory.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Bias-Variance Tradeoff in Small-Sample Regimes}

The UCI concrete dataset, with 1{,}030 samples, sits in a regime where the bias-variance tradeoff strongly favors simpler models. Each additional feature, model, or parameter introduces variance that must be offset by bias reduction to yield net improvement. For gradient-boosted trees on 8 raw features, the $R^2 \approx 0.95$ achieved suggests that bias is already low; remaining error is dominated by irreducible noise (measurement error, unobserved mix variables, inherent material variability). In this regime, architectural complexity primarily adds variance, explaining why V4 (raw features, simple ensemble) outperforms V3 (engineered features, complex ensemble).

This observation generalizes: for tabular datasets with $N < 10^4$ and $D < 20$, a single well-tuned gradient-boosted tree model is likely to approach the empirical performance ceiling. Additional architectural complexity should be adopted only after demonstrating that a tuned single model leaves statistically significant headroom.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Generalization}

A critical question for any negative-result study is whether the conclusions are specific to the dataset analyzed. The ablation findings presented here are validated across two independent concrete datasets that differ in four substantive dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Geography and material system:} International mixes (UCI) vs.\ Mexican parametric studies with CEMEX/Apasco brands and Opuntia ficus indica natural additive.
\item \textbf{Feature space:} Pure mix-design variables plus age (UCI) vs.\ mix design augmented with NDT measurements including UPV, electrical resistivity, tensile strength, and flexural strength (Mexican).
\item \textbf{Sample size:} 1{,}030 (UCI) vs.\ 4{,}420 (Mexican).
\item \textbf{Baseline predictability:} AdaBoost $R^2 = 0.9142$ (UCI) vs.\ $R^2 = 0.9936$ (Mexican), with the latter's high baseline driven by the UPV NDT proxy.
\end{enumerate}

Despite these differences, the qualitative ablation pattern is identical: model upgrade dominates, feature engineering is counterproductive or neutral, multi-model ensembles are functionally redundant, age-conditioned piecewise blending provides negligible benefit, and constrained optimization is non-binding. The convergence of findings across such different datasets strongly supports the generalizability of the central conclusion.

The key insight transcends the specific domain of concrete strength prediction: when a direct physical proxy (such as UPV) or a strong non-linear learner (GBDT) already captures the dominant variance, architectural complexity adds estimation variance without bias reduction. This is a statistical phenomenon---a manifestation of the bias-variance tradeoff in finite samples---not a peculiarity of concrete mix design data. It suggests that the burden of proof should shift: in ML-for-engineering applications, the default should be a single, rigorously tuned model, with complex architectures adopted only after ablation demonstrates statistically significant marginal gain over that baseline.

\subsection{ASTM Noise Ceiling Analysis}

A natural question is whether the $R^2$ values achieved in this study are limited by measurement noise in the compressive strength target. ASTM C39/C39M-21 \cite{astm2021c39} specifies an intra-laboratory coefficient of variation of approximately 4.0\% for individual cylinder tests under standard conditions. This measurement noise imposes a theoretical upper bound on achievable $R^2$: even a perfect model of the true (noise-free) strength would be penalized by the variance of the measurement process. Based on the ASTM intra-lab precision, the maximum achievable $R^2$ given measurement noise alone is approximately 0.9984.

The $R^2$ values achieved by the best models---0.9523 on UCI and 0.9954 on the Mexican dataset---are well below this ceiling. The gap to $R^2 = 1.0$ is therefore dominated by factors other than measurement precision, namely finite-sample estimation error and missing predictor variables (e.g., aggregate gradation, curing humidity, cement chemistry). Measurement noise accounts for less than 0.16\% of the $R^2$ gap on both datasets.

To quantify the statistical uncertainty around these point estimates, bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals were computed for the best-performing variant (V4) on both datasets:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{UCI V4:} $R^2 = 0.9530$ [0.9406, 0.9622], interval width $= 0.0215$
\item \textbf{Mexican V4:} $R^2 = 0.9952$ [0.9948, 0.9955], interval width $= 0.0007$ (31$\times$ narrower)
\end{itemize}

The 31-fold narrower bootstrap CI on the Mexican dataset reflects both the larger sample size ($N=4{,}420$ vs.\ 1{,}030) and the lower residual variance due to the UPV predictor. On both datasets, approximately 96.6\% of the gap to $R^2 = 1.0$ is attributable to finite-sample error and missing predictor variables rather than to measurement noise. Fig.~\ref{fig:bootstrap_ci} visualizes the bootstrap distributions.

These results have a direct implication for model design: measurement noise is \emph{not} the limiting factor. The practical ceiling is set by the availability of predictor variables that encode genuinely independent physical information not already captured by the existing feature set.

\subsection{Age-Stratified Error Analysis}

The age-conditioning motivation in ACDCB rests on the assumption that predictive difficulty differs across age regimes due to changing hydration kinetics. To test this, we decompose prediction error by age group.

\subsubsection{UCI Age-Stratified Results}

On the UCI dataset, a clear age-dependent pattern emerges:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Early age (1--7 days):} $R^2 = 0.903$, with the highest RMSE coefficient of variation (CV) at 24.2\%.
\item \textbf{Late age (90--365 days):} $R^2 = 0.969$, with substantially lower error.
\end{itemize}

The early-age regime is indeed harder to predict, consistent with the rapid, nonlinear strength gain during the first week of curing and the higher variability in mix proportion effects at early ages.

\subsubsection{Mexican Dataset Age-Stratified Results}

The Mexican dataset, with its balanced age sampling, reveals a different pattern:
\begin{itemize}
\item Day 3: $R^2 = 0.997$ (highest)
\item Day 60: $R^2 = 0.973$ (lowest)
\item $R^2$ coefficient of variation across ages: 0.8\%
\end{itemize}

The near-uniform performance across the 8 discrete age levels reflects the balanced sampling and the dominating influence of the UPV predictor, which is strongly correlated with strength at all ages. The day-60 dip is small in absolute terms and likely reflects a modest increase in material variability at intermediate ages rather than a fundamental shift in predictability.

\subsubsection{Implications}

Two conclusions follow. First, age-dependent error patterns exist but their magnitude and character are dataset-specific: UCI shows a clear early-age penalty, while the Mexican dataset shows near-uniform performance. Second, and more importantly for the ablation findings, \textbf{age-dependent error is not the cause of component failure}. The age-conditioned piecewise blending fails not because age is irrelevant, but because gradient-boosted trees already learn age-conditioned splits natively. The marginal gain from explicit age partitioning is negligible even on UCI, where age-dependent error is pronounced. Fig.~\ref{fig:age_stratified} provides the age-stratified error visualization.

\subsection{Simplicity and Interpretability in Engineering Practice}

Beyond predictive accuracy, structural engineering applications impose requirements that favor simpler models:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Interpretability}: A single XGBoost model permits feature importance ranking, SHAP dependence plots, and partial dependence analysis through well-established tooling. A multi-model ensemble with piecewise age-conditioned weights obscures these analyses behind an additional layer of fusion.

\item \textbf{Deployability}: A single model requires one serialized object; ACDCB requires four base models plus weight vectors and age-threshold logic. In production quality-control settings, simpler deployment reduces integration risk.

\item \textbf{Maintainability}: When a single model's prediction is anomalous, feature attribution identifies the responsible input. When a 4-model ensemble's prediction is anomalous, the diagnostician must additionally determine which model's contribution drove the error.
\end{itemize}

These practical considerations, combined with the empirical finding that ACDCB underperforms a single tuned XGBoost, lead to a clear recommendation: \textbf{rigorous single-model optimization should be the default baseline in structural engineering ML, with architectural complexity reserved for cases where ablation demonstrates statistically significant marginal gain.}

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations of this study warrant acknowledgement:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Dataset scope}: The UCI dataset, while widely used as a benchmark, comprises only 1{,}030 samples with 8 base variables. The Mexican validation dataset extends the sample size to 4{,}420 and introduces NDT predictors, but both datasets are limited to concrete compressive strength. The finding that feature engineering is counterproductive may not generalize to larger, higher-dimensional datasets where engineered features encode genuinely independent physical information.

\item \textbf{Heterogeneous model scope}: The additional MLP/GPR test reduces cross-prediction correlations but remains limited to standard tabular learners and the same input variables. More expressive heterogeneous designs or models using genuinely independent data modalities may behave differently.

\item \textbf{Age-conditioning mechanism}: Soft age-gating via sigmoid weighting was tested and found to provide no measurable improvement ($\Delta R^2 < 0.0001$), confirming that the hard threshold choice is not the limiting factor. Even data-driven smooth transitions in the age-gating mechanism provide no predictive gain.

\item \textbf{Hyperparameter sensitivity}: While models were tuned via Bayesian optimization (200--500 trials), the interaction between feature engineering, hyperparameters, and ensembling was not jointly optimized due to combinatorial explosion. It is theoretically possible that a specific hyperparameter regime exists where engineered features outperform raw features, though the monotonic relationship observed in stepwise ablation and the cross-dataset replication make this unlikely.

\item \textbf{Uncertainty quantification}: Split conformal prediction \cite{angelopoulos2021conformal} was evaluated on the Mexican dataset to test whether the ensemble framework improves uncertainty quantification even when point predictions are redundant. At 95\% confidence, both the ACDCB ensemble and a single XGBoost model achieved a prediction interval coverage probability (PICP) of approximately 0.93. The ensemble produced slightly wider intervals ($+0.076$\,MPa mean prediction interval width), indicating that model diversity does not translate into more efficient uncertainty quantification. Fig.~\ref{fig:conformal} compares the conformal prediction intervals.

\item \textbf{Generalizability to other engineering domains}: While the cross-dataset validation within concrete strength prediction is encouraging, the findings are specific to this domain. The central mechanism---a strong learner saturating the achievable headroom, leaving negligible room for architectural complexity---is likely to apply wherever tabular datasets have modest dimensionality and strong baseline predictability, but empirical verification in other structural engineering tasks (e.g., deflection prediction, crack width estimation, seismic response) is needed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Engineering Application Potential}

For practicing structural engineers, the practical implication is straightforward: a single, well-tuned XGBoost model trained on raw mix design variables and curing age provides state-of-the-art concrete strength prediction with minimal implementation complexity. The model can be deployed as a spreadsheet-embeddable function or a lightweight web API, requiring only the 8 standard mix design inputs (or the equivalent input set for non-UCI data). Hyperparameter tuning via automated Bayesian optimization (200 trials, approximately 5 minutes on standard hardware) is sufficient to reach the performance plateau identified in this study. We recommend this ``tuned single model'' approach as the pragmatic standard for concrete strength prediction in both research benchmarking and engineering practice.

%==========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%==========================================================================

This study constructed and systematically dissected ACDCB---a four-component ensemble framework for concrete compressive strength prediction---to determine whether each architectural addition provides statistically significant marginal value over a single, well-tuned gradient-boosted tree model. The framework is used here as a controlled ablation vehicle, not as a recommended prediction architecture. The findings, validated across two independent datasets (UCI, $N = 1{,}030$; Mexican, $N = 4{,}420$), are summarized as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The appearance of strong performance is driven by the baseline choice.} ACDCB's 25.6\% RMSE reduction over AdaBoost on UCI ($R^2 = 0.949$ vs.\ $0.914$) is almost entirely attributable to replacing AdaBoost with gradient-boosted trees ($\Delta R^2 \approx +0.038$), not to the framework's architectural innovations. On the Mexican dataset, the AdaBoost baseline already achieves $R^2 = 0.9936$, reducing the headroom for any method to $\Delta R^2 < 0.007$.

\item \textbf{Feature engineering is counterproductive.} The 32-dimensional engineered feature space introduces severe collinearity, causing engineered-feature models to underperform raw-feature counterparts ($\Delta R^2 = -0.005$). VIF filtering and LASSO ranking improve upon the full engineered space but still fail to match the raw 8D baseline. On the Mexican dataset, LASSO identifies only 3 of 32 features with non-zero coefficients (all UPV-derived), and restricting to LASSO top-12 degrades performance relative to raw features.

\item \textbf{The multi-model pool is functionally redundant.} Cross-model OOF prediction correlations exceed 0.996 for all pairs, rendering ensemble fusion nearly indistinguishable from single-model prediction. Adding MLP/GPR members lowers the minimum pairwise correlation to approximately 0.97 but still yields only $\Delta R^2 \leq +0.0013$ over the best single pool member.

\item \textbf{Age-conditioned piecewise blending is unnecessary.} A scan of age thresholds $\tau \in [3, 180]$ days yields an $R^2$ range of $4.2 \times 10^{-4}$---complete flatness. Sigmoid soft-weighting tested with $\kappa \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0\}$ yields $\Delta R^2 < 0.0001$ on both datasets, confirming that neither the hard threshold choice nor the binary partitioning structure is the limiting factor.

\item \textbf{Constrained optimization is benign but non-contributory.} OLS without constraints yields near-identical weights and predictions on both datasets, confirming that the convex constraint set is non-binding in well-calibrated settings.

\item \textbf{Cross-dataset validation confirms generalizability.} The identical ablation pattern across two independent datasets---differing in geography, material system, feature space, sample size (4.3$\times$), and baseline predictability---provides strong evidence that the findings reflect a general statistical phenomenon rather than artifacts of the UCI benchmark. The 4{,}420-sample Mexican dataset yields the same conclusion with even smaller margins: all complex components contribute $\Delta R^2 \leq 0.002$.

\item \textbf{A single fine-tuned XGBoost model is the empirical performance ceiling.} XGBoost with Bayesian-optimized hyperparameters on raw features achieves $R^2 = 0.9523$, RMSE $= 3.65$\,MPa on UCI, and $R^2 = 0.9954$, RMSE $= 0.6464$\,MPa on the Mexican dataset, outperforming the full ACDCB framework on both datasets with lower complexity.
\end{enumerate}

These findings do not invalidate the individual design principles underlying ACDCB---dual representations, constrained fusion, and age-awareness remain conceptually sound. Rather, they demonstrate that on small-to-medium tabular datasets typical of structural engineering benchmarks, these principles do not translate into measurable predictive gains beyond what a single, well-optimized learner provides. We conclude that rigorous hyperparameter optimization of a single strong model should be the mandatory baseline in this domain, and that additional architectural complexity must be justified through ablation demonstrating statistically significant marginal improvement---not assumed from plausible motivation alone.
